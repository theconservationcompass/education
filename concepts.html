Conceptualising Conservation

Conservation addresses the critical issue of biodiversity loss and the resulting breakdown of ecosystems. It involves a comprehensive process of evaluating the state of biodiversity, taking action to protect species and habitats, and monitoring efforts to ensure success.

Biodiversity refers to the variety of animals, plants, and microorganisms that live within a particular habitat. These habitats, whether they are forests, oceans, meadows, or wetlands, provide the conditions that organisms need to survive and thrive. All these parts work together, interacting within an ecosystem—a complex network of life where each species plays a role in maintaining balance. However, when biodiversity is lost, ecosystems start to break down, and this can have cascading effects on the environment, human societies, and the planet's health.

Conservationists face the challenge of addressing biodiversity loss on a global scale with limited resources. This can be overwhelming: Where do we start? What should we prioritise? Conservation biologists aim to identify strategies which maximise positive outcomes—ensuring that species are protected, ecosystems healthy, and the essential services they provide continue for future generations.

Biodiversity, the variety of life on Earth, is the foundation of balanced and self-sustaining ecosystems. These ecosystems are interconnected networks where every species plays a role. For instance, bees pollinate crops, trees produce oxygen, and large herbivores maintain the landscape. When all parts of an ecosystem are healthy, the system as a whole functions smoothly. This interconnectedness means that conserving biodiversity doesn’t just protect individual species, it helps to maintain the resilience of entire ecosystems.

One of the most important aspects of biodiversity is that it strengthens ecosystems' ability to withstand and adapt to environmental changes. Higher biodiversity means more genetic variation and a greater diversity of life forms, which improves the chances that some species will survive challenges like disease, natural disasters, or human disturbances. In a diverse ecosystem, if some species are impacted by environmental stressors, others may still thrive, helping to keep the system balanced and enabling it to recover more quickly.

This resilience is critical not only for nature but for human society as well. Healthy ecosystems provide essential services—clean air, fresh water, pollination of crops, and much more—that we rely on every day. Protecting biodiversity means protecting the ecosystems that provide us with the resources we rely on.

To effectively protect species and their habitats, it's crucial to understand the key threats they face. During the 1992 Rio Earth Summit, the five greatest threats to global biodiversity were identified, and they remain significant challenges today:

  • Climate Change: The rapid increase in global temperatures, driven by greenhouse gas emissions, is disrupting ecosystems around the world. Rising temperatures, shifting weather patterns, and extreme events like droughts and storms are affecting species' ability to survive in their natural habitats. Climate change also creates cascading effects, altering food chains and forcing species to adapt or migrate faster than they can cope.

  • Invasive Species: Human activities have accelerated the global movement of species, often introducing non-native species into new environments. These invasive species can outcompete native wildlife for resources, spread diseases, or disrupt ecosystems in ways that harm local biodiversity. Once established, invasive species can be difficult to control and may lead to the extinction of native species.

  • Pollution: Human-caused pollution, from chemicals to noise, throws ecosystems out of balance. Pesticides, plastics, and industrial waste contaminate water, air, and soil, harming wildlife and plants. Even non-chemical pollution, such as noise or light pollution, can interfere with natural behaviors like mating, migration, and feeding, negatively impacting biodiversity.

  • Deforestation and Overexploitation: The overuse of natural resources is putting immense pressure on species and habitats. Deforestation for timber, agriculture, and urban expansion is destroying forests that serve as vital homes for countless species. Overfishing, hunting, and logging are depleting wildlife populations faster than they can recover, pushing many species toward extinction.

  • Land and Sea Use Change: The conversion of land for agriculture, cities, and infrastructure is one of the biggest drivers of biodiversity loss. As natural habitats are cleared for farming, housing, or industry, ecosystems are destroyed or fragmented, leaving species with less space to live and fewer resources to survive. Similarly, human activities in the ocean, such as overfishing or coastal development, threaten marine ecosystems.

These threats are often interconnected, compounding the challenges that species and ecosystems face. By recognising and addressing these issues, conservation efforts can target the most critical areas to protect biodiversity and maintain the balance of life on Earth.

Conservation efforts generally follow two main approaches: species-focused conservation and habitat-focused conservation. Each has its own strengths, and both are crucial in preserving biodiversity.

Species-Focused Conservation: This approach targets individual species, especially those at high risk of extinction. By focusing efforts on protecting a single species, conservationists aim to prevent its decline and ensure its survival. Species-focused strategies often involve breeding programs, legal protection, and habitat restoration efforts tailored to the specific needs of the species in question.

An example of this approach is the protection of charismatic species like tigers, pandas, or rhinos. These efforts can galvanise public support and funding, while also benefiting the broader ecosystems where these species live.

Within this approach, particular attention is given to keystone species—species that have a disproportionately large impact on their ecosystems. The loss of a keystone species can trigger the collapse or severe degradation of the ecosystem it supports. For example, the loss of a predator like the wolf in certain areas has caused overpopulation of prey species, which disrupts vegetation and other animal populations. Understanding which species play this critical role in your region is important for guiding local conservation efforts (for example, using the data dashboard to identify keystone species).

Another key focus is on edge species, which are genetically distinct and often at the fringes of their evolutionary lineages. Conserving these species is important for maximizing genetic diversity and ensuring the resilience of biodiversity as a whole. By protecting edge species, we safeguard the evolutionary potential of life on Earth, which may be critical in adapting to future environmental changes.



Habitat-Focused Conservation: In contrast, habitat-focused conservation aims to protect entire ecosystems, ensuring that all the species within them have the resources they need to thrive. By safeguarding habitats such as wetlands, forests, or coral reefs, this approach ensures that ecosystems remain healthy and functional. A well-preserved habitat provides shelter, food, and breeding grounds for a wide range of species, both known and unknown. Habitat conservation efforts often involve creating protected areas, promoting sustainable land use, and restoring degraded environments.

A prime example of this strategy is the conservation of biodiversity hotspots—regions with the greatest levels of biodiversity. These areas are often found in tropical regions, where stable temperatures year-round allow species to focus their energy on reproduction rather than adapting to extreme seasonal changes. This consistent environment, along with higher productivity, has led to greater speciation over time, making these hotspots critical to global biodiversity. Because biodiversity hotspots contain such high concentrations of species, some argue that prioritizing the protection of these areas offers the most efficient use of conservation resources. However, focusing solely on these fewer areas raises important questions: Should we concentrate our attention on safeguarding biodiversity hotspots, or should we spread our efforts across a wider range of habitats, including those with lower biodiversity but unique species?


Ultimately, species-focused and habitat-focused conservation are complementary. Protecting a keystone species often involves safeguarding its habitat, and preserving a rich ecosystem in a biodiversity hotspot benefits countless species, including those that may not yet be recognized as critically important. Effective conservation strategies often blend both approaches to maximize impact.

Ecosystem services are the direct and indirect benefits that humans receive from nature. These services are often categorised into four main types:

  1. Provisioning Services: These are products directly obtained from ecosystems, such as food, water, timber, and medicine. Healthy ecosystems ensure that these resources are sustainably available.
  2. Regulating Services: Ecosystems regulate climate, water, and disease, providing stability. For example, forests absorb carbon dioxide, reducing the effects of climate change, while wetlands filter pollutants from water.
  3. Supporting Services: These are fundamental to all other ecosystem services, such as soil formation, photosynthesis, and nutrient cycling. They maintain the conditions for life on Earth.
  4. Cultural Services: Beyond material benefits, nature provides cultural, spiritual, and recreational value. Natural spaces inspire art, provide places for leisure, and support mental and emotional well-being.

The degradation of ecosystems undermines these services, impacting everything from food security to climate stability. Conservation works to maintain and restore ecosystems so they can continue to provide these essential services.

Conservation is not just about protecting nature—it’s also about ensuring the sustainability of human societies. Sustainable development is the idea that we can meet today’s needs without compromising the ability of future generations to meet theirs. Conservation is essential to this goal, as it ensures that ecosystems continue to function and provide the services that human societies rely on. Whether it’s protecting forests that purify air and water, or conserving marine ecosystems that provide fish for billions of people, safeguarding biodiversity is fundamental to sustainable development. Without healthy ecosystems and species, achieving sustainability will be impossible, as our economic and social well-being is intrinsically linked to the natural systems around us.

International initiatives like the UN Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) highlight the role of conservation in reducing poverty, ensuring food and water security, and promoting well-being. For example, SDG 15 specifically calls for the protection of life on land by halting biodiversity loss and managing forests, wetlands, and ecosystems sustainably. Similarly, SDG 14 focuses on the conservation of marine ecosystems, which are critical for the food security and livelihoods of millions of people globally.

Through conservation, we ensure that natural resources are used wisely and that ecosystems remain functional. This means finding a balance between development and environmental protection—working with communities, governments, and industries to integrate conservation into everyday practices, from farming and forestry to urban planning.

Conservation also supports many other SDGs. For example, protecting biodiversity is closely tied to goals related to health, since natural ecosystems provide the basis for traditional medicine and modern pharmaceuticals. Similarly, conservation supports food security, as diverse and resilient ecosystems are vital for sustainable agriculture and fisheries. By maintaining the balance of life on land and below water, we help secure the foundation for sustainable human development across a wide range of sectors.
